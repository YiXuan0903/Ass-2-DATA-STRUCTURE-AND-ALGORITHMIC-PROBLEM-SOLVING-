Popular Dishes in Rome

Pasta alla Carbonara

Fry thick-cut guanciale (cured pork cheek) until the fat renders. In a separate bowl, whisk together egg yolks and plenty of Pecorino Romano cheese, seasoning with a generous amount of black pepper. Combine everything with freshly cooked, drained pasta (spaghetti, tonnarelli, or rigatoni work best), ensuring the heat is off to create a creamy, rich sauce without scrambling the eggs.

Carciofi alla Romana e Carciofi alla Giudia (Roman and Jewish artichokes)

There are 2 ways to cook this cuisine. The first style is called Carciofi alla Romana—artichokes are trimmed, stuffed with mint, garlic, and parsley, then gently braised in a mixture of olive oil, white wine, and water. As the liquid evaporates, the artichokes become tender and infused with the delicate flavors of the cooking wine. The second style is Carciofi alla Giudia—the Jewish-style artichoke, synonymous with Rome’s historic Jewish Quarter. After careful trimming, the artichoke is flattened so its leaves open like a flower, then deep-fried whole. When it emerges from the hot oil, it is both crispy and golden on the outside, yet soft and flavorful on the inside.

Maritozzi

A large, fluffy sweet bread, filled with lightly sweetened fresh cream and often enriched with pine nuts, raisins, and candied orange peel.
